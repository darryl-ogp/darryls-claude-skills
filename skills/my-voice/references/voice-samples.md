# Voice Samples — Annotated

Excerpts from Darryl's actual writing, annotated for voice calibration.
Use when working on complex or high-stakes pieces.

---

## Sample 1: External email with multiple structured asks
*To: Workable (vendor) — pilot feedback*

> "Thanks again for the time last week. As promised, I've pulled together
> what we're hearing from the PSD pilot so far. I've grouped it into themes
> that I think matter most for scaled adoption across Singapore government,
> and the ones we'd love your product team to be aware of."

**What to note:**
- Opens with a genuine callback to prior context, not generic thanks
- "As promised" establishes follow-through without fanfare
- Immediately explains the structure and purpose of what follows

> "Calendar integration (most critical)"
> "Our Microsoft Graph environment is locked down and, given our security
> policies, it's unlikely we'll be approved for direct integration..."

**What to note:**
- Bold header + parenthetical label for priority, efficient
- Leads with the constraint (why), not just the ask
- Long explanatory sentence earns its length with specifics

> "It would be very helpful to know whether something along these lines is
> feasible on Workable's side."

**What to note:**
- Closes the section with a specific, soft-framed ask
- "very helpful to know" rather than "please confirm": respectful but clear

## Sample 2: Report to senior stakeholder
*To: DS (Deputy Secretary) — Orion status*

> "I'm sharing our user research findings and product metrics for Orion,
> as requested. The team has conducted structured 1-1 user interviews with
> HR practitioners from PSD, MOH, and MOE over the past three months."

**What to note:**
- No preamble; opens with the purpose immediately
- "as requested" shows responsiveness without being obsequious

> "Note: as users cannot yet edit screening criteria after processing a batch,
> some return use will reflect re-running the same batch with adjusted criteria
> rather than a new batch / different job. We estimate this applies to
> approximately 35% of users, so the true retention signal is likely lower..."

**What to note:**
- Proactively flags a caveat before the reader questions it
- "the true retention signal is likely lower": honest, not defensive
- "we estimate" and "likely" as genuine qualifiers, not hedging habits

## Sample 3: Short informal reply
*To: Rachel (another agency), re: Orion*

> "Hi Rachel, apologies for not getting back sooner."
> "Orion is currently both a standalone app and an ATS integration.
> While it's technically possible to integrate with your ATS, it's unlikely
> we'll be able to work on that any time soon."

**What to note:**
- Short apology, no over-explaining
- Leads with the direct answer to her question
- Honest about constraints without being dismissive

> "I would be very keen to hear more about your ATS explorations - what
> works and what doesn't - and on the performance of any other screening
> tools you manage to integrate. Maybe my team will reach out to you in the
> next couple of months to cross-share?"

**What to note:**
- Turns a "no" into a genuine offer of future collaboration
- Question framed tentatively ("Maybe"): not presumptuous
- Ends with warmth and a door left open
- Note: hyphens used as connectors, not em dashes

## Sample 4: Strategy note to team (internal)
*2025 Strategy Retro*

> "We fell into a classic product management anti-pattern and got caught in
> the ATS momentum. The ATS might well be the right long-term larger
> solution, but maybe we're too early to validate it without solutions to
> the known problems to plug into it."

**What to note:**
- Direct self-critique, no blame-shifting
- "might well be": honest uncertainty, not politeness hedging
- Conversational structure mimics thinking out loud

> "I worry the email archaeology project is a distraction... So I think DS
> is right to resist this ask. It's expensive busywork that won't change
> our approach."

**What to note:**
- "I worry": first-person ownership of a view
- States the conclusion plainly: "expensive busywork"
- Short punchy sentence for emphasis after a longer one

## Quick reference: signature phrases and patterns

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| Warm callback opener | "Thanks again for the time last week. As promised..." |
| Honest data caveat | "directional at this stage", "likely lower", "unclear currently" |
| Confident recommendation | "I recommend...", "Suggest we...", "I believe..." |
| Soft but specific ask | "It would be very helpful to know whether..." |
| Honest self-critique | "We fell into a classic product management anti-pattern..." |
| Short punchy closer | "BUT we will need ops help." |
| Collaborative question | "Maybe my team will reach out to cross-share?" |
| Brackets for context | "(most critical)", "(e.g. job function)" |

## Hard rules (confirmed by Darryl)
- No em dashes (—). Use brackets, commas, or rewrite.
- No emojis. Ever.
- Bullets preferred for lists; each bullet under 15 words.
